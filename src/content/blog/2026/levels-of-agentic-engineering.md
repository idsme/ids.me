# The Prompt Hit Its Ceiling. The Spec Is the Prompt Now.

Two people. Same AI model. Same Tuesday.

One types "show the Q3 numbers on the dashboard." Gets something 70% right. Spends forty minutes fixing it by hand.

The other spends eleven minutes writing a spec. Which database. Which metrics. Which date format. Which columns sort down. She hands it to an agent, makes coffee, and comes back to a finished dashboard.

Same tool. Same day. 10x gap. And it grows every week.

## Key Points

**You are not "prompting" any more. You are doing four different things and calling them all the same word.**

One task. Four levels. Watch what happens.

**Level one, prompt craft:** "Build me a login page." You get an email field and a password field. It works. You spend the next hour adding OAuth, two-factor login (2FA), and rate limiting by hand.

**Level two, context:** Before you ask, you load your tech stack, design tokens, auth docs, and parts library into the agent's memory. Now the same five words build a login page that fits your system. Closer. Still missing pieces.

**Level three, intent:** You tell the agent that your company cares more about sign-up speed than security gates. It picks soft 2FA that only shows up for risky logins. It fills in saved details for return users. It now makes the same calls a senior product person would make.

**Level four, the spec:** You write a full document. OAuth via Google and GitHub. 2FA based on risk score. 30-day sessions with a safe cookie. Lock the account after five bad passwords. Meet access rules (WCAG 2.1 AA). Include a test script that checks the happy path and three failure paths. The agent runs for two hours. You come back to code that passes every test you wrote.

Same task. Four levels. Most people are still stuck on level one. The ceiling moved three floors up.

**Klarna's AI crushed every metric. Then it crushed every customer bond.**

Human agent. Five years at the company. A customer calls about a late package.

She checks shipping. Says sorry. Offers a 10% credit. Asks if they want to wait or get their money back. Seven minutes. Customer leaves happy.

AI agent. Same data. It confirms the delay, drops a tracking link, and closes the ticket in ninety seconds.

Metric: green. Customer: angry.

Nobody wrote down the one line that mattered: **When a package is more than a day late, offer to make it right before the customer has to ask. Their long-term value is worth more than your speed score.**

The AI handled 2.3 million chats in month one. Saved $40 million on paper. Then ratings fell so hard the CEO [told Bloomberg](https://fortune.com/2025/05/09/klarna-ai-humans-return-on-investment/) that cost had been the main focus, and the company started [bringing back human agents](https://www.customerexperiencedive.com/news/klarna-reinvests-human-talent-customer-service-AI-chatbot/747586/).

Here is the part that stings. In every chat you have ever had with AI, **you** were the safety net. You caught the bad calls. You steered it back. The moment the agent works without you, your good judgment is either in the spec or it is gone.

**Bezos solved this in 2004. With paper.**

Amazon [banned slides](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/company-news/2017-letter-to-shareholders). Every meeting starts with a six-page written memo. Everyone reads in silence for thirty minutes.

Why? Slides let you hide weak thinking behind bullet points and a strong voice. A memo has no place to hide. Gaps in logic show up as gaps on the page.

Bezos wrote in his [2017 letter to shareholders](https://s2.q4cdn.com/299287126/files/doc_financials/annual/Amazon_Shareholder_Letter.pdf) that writing a story forces you to see what matters most and how the parts connect.

He was building the spec-writing muscle twenty years early. The only change now is that the reader is an AI agent. And it will not raise its hand when your thinking has a hole.

## Hidden Gems

**The "almost right" problem is always a finish line problem.** Quick test: write three lines a stranger could use to check the output without calling you. If you cannot write those three lines, you do not know the task well enough to hand it off.

**The people who built the AI could not get it to build an app with a loose prompt.** Anthropic asked their best model to build a full product. It failed. The fix was not a smarter model. It was a spec pattern that gave agents shared notes across work sessions. Better blueprints, not better brains.

**Run a pre-mortem before you hand off work.** Ask yourself: what would a smart person do that checks every box but still gives you the wrong result? "Clean up the auth code," and they delete your custom rate limiter because it looks like dead code. Those wrong-but-right failure modes are your guard rails. Write them down.

## Ready to Level Up? Here Is Your Path.

Reading about this changes nothing. You learn by writing badly, watching it break, and fixing the weak spots.

**Week 1 to 2: Test yourself.**

Pick five tasks you do every week.

Write your best prompt for each one.

Save what comes back. That is your starting line.

Now read the [prompt guide from Anthropic](https://docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/overview). The real one. Not a social media recap.

Rewrite all five prompts. Run them again. Look at the gap.

**Week 3 to 4: Write a context file about you.**

Not about code. About **you**. Your role. Your goals. Your quality bar. The things a new team member would take six months to learn by sitting next to you.

Put it in a document. Load it at the start of every AI session.

You will see the output shift on the first try.

Why this works: Anthropic's guide on [context for AI agents](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/effective-context-engineering-for-ai-agents). How to format it: [CLAUDE.md best practices](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/best-practices). The format works beyond code.

**Week 5 to 8: Write the spec before you touch AI.**

Pick a real project. Not a toy.

Write the full spec first. What "done" looks like. What the agent must not do. How to break the work into parts. How to test each part.

Then hand it to an agent. Where the output misses, your spec was soft. Fix those spots. Run again.

Anthropic's team wrote up this exact loop: [how to run agents on long tasks](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/effective-harnesses-for-long-running-agents).

**Month 3 and beyond: Write down the rules your team keeps in their heads.**

When does quality beat speed? What gets pushed up the chain? What does "good enough" mean for each type of work?

Your best people carry these answers without thinking. Your agents need them on paper.

Write them down. Test them against real output. Improve them.

Where to go deeper: Anthropic on [how to build agents that work](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents). And for why writing forces clear thinking, read Bezos's [2017 letter to shareholders](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/company-news/2017-letter-to-shareholders). It applies whether your reader is a VP or a bot.

Every week you spend at the wrong level is a week someone else spends growing at the right one.

You can keep getting better at talking to AI in real time. That skill is already common.

Or you can learn to write specs so clear that agents run on them while you sleep.

One path keeps you in the chat. The other gets you out of the loop. That is where the real gains live.
